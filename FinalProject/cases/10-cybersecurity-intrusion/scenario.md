# Scenario 10: Cybersecurity Intrusion Detection

## Business Context

Apex Financial Technologies is a fintech firm with approximately 500 employees that processes payment transactions for mid-market e-commerce platforms. The company handles over $2 billion in annual transaction volume and stores sensitive customer financial data subject to PCI-DSS, SOC 2, and state privacy regulations. In the past two years, two peer companies of similar size suffered data breaches that resulted in regulatory fines exceeding $10 million each, massive customer notification costs, and in one case, the complete loss of a major client relationship.

The Chief Information Security Officer (CISO) has tasked the security engineering team with developing a machine learning model to augment the existing network intrusion detection system (IDS). The current system relies primarily on signature-based detection, which is effective against known attack patterns but misses zero-day exploits, novel lateral movement techniques, and slow-and-low data exfiltration campaigns. The ML model will analyze network flow data in near-real-time, scoring each connection for anomaly indicators and routing high-risk connections to the Security Operations Center (SOC) for manual triage.

The dataset contains network connection records labeled as either normal traffic or confirmed intrusions, based on retrospective forensic analysis. Intrusions are extremely rare -- approximately 1.5% of connections -- making this one of the most severely imbalanced classification tasks in the scenario set. The SOC has limited analyst capacity, and each investigated alert consumes roughly one hour of a senior analyst's time, costing approximately $2,000 in loaded labor. More critically, alert fatigue from excessive false alarms degrades the SOC's ability to respond to genuine threats: studies show that SOC analysts begin ignoring alerts when false positive rates exceed 50%.

The potential cost of a missed intrusion is catastrophic. A successful data breach at Apex would trigger regulatory fines, mandatory customer notification, forensic investigation costs, legal liability, and potential loss of PCI-DSS certification (which would halt the company's ability to process payments). The CISO estimates the total cost of a breach at $40,000 as a conservative lower bound, recognizing that a severe breach involving customer financial data could cost several million dollars.

## Key Stakeholders

- **Chief Information Security Officer (CISO):** Ultimately responsible for the company's security posture; reports directly to the CEO and Board on cyber risk.
- **Security Operations Center (SOC) Analysts:** Triage alerts generated by the model; need high-quality alerts with low false positive rates to maintain effectiveness.
- **Network Engineering:** Manages the infrastructure that generates the connection data; can implement automated blocking for very-high-confidence detections.
- **Compliance/Legal:** Ensures the company meets its regulatory obligations; a breach would trigger mandatory incident reporting.
- **Executive Leadership/Board:** Views cybersecurity as an existential business risk; has authorized significant investment in detection capabilities.

## Cost Structure

| Prediction | Actual | Outcome | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| Normal (0) | Normal (0) | True Negative | $0 |
| Intrusion (1) | Intrusion (1) | True Positive | $2,000 (SOC analyst investigation; threat contained) |
| Intrusion (1) | Normal (0) | **False Positive** | **$2,000** (SOC analyst investigates false alarm, contributing to alert fatigue) |
| Normal (0) | Intrusion (1) | **False Negative** | **$40,000** (data breach: fines, notification, legal, business disruption) |

## Special Considerations

- The FN/FP cost ratio is 5,000:1. This is by far the most extreme cost asymmetry in the entire scenario set. Missing a single intrusion could cost as much as 5,000 false alarms. This should dramatically influence threshold selection.
- The class imbalance is extreme (~1.5% positive rate). Combined with the astronomical FN cost, this scenario rewards students who deeply understand the interplay between imbalance handling, threshold optimization, and cost-sensitive evaluation.
- No single feature is a reliable intrusion indicator. Intrusions are characterized by combinations of features: high error rates with many same-host connections suggest scanning; failed logins on guest accounts suggest credential stuffing; long connections with large outbound data suggest exfiltration.
- The serror_rate (SYN error rate) is a key feature but only becomes informative when combined with connection count -- a high error rate on one connection is noise, but a high error rate across many connections to the same host is a strong scanning signature.
- Failed logins have a multiplicative interaction with guest login attempts: multiple failed logins on guest accounts are far more suspicious than failed logins on regular accounts.
- Several features are relatively uninformative: src_port_ephemeral, dst_host_count, and protocol_type have weak or no real relationship with intrusion status in this dataset.
- The extremely low positive rate means that standard metrics like accuracy are almost meaningless (a model predicting all-zeros achieves 98.5% accuracy). Evaluation must focus on cost-sensitive metrics, recall at acceptable precision levels, or PR-AUC.
- Alert fatigue is a real operational concern: even if the cost math justifies a very low threshold, the SOC cannot effectively process thousands of false alarms per day. Students should consider practical operational constraints alongside pure cost optimization.
